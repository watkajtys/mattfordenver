Matt Watkajtys for Denver Mayor

Who? Matt Watkajtys (Wa-Kite-Us)

Matt has rented in the Golden Triangle in Denver since 2017. He worked as a product development consultant before covid. When Covid hit, Matt's income fell off a cliff. He applied for unemployment. He is currently utilizing the Emergency Rent Relief program in 2021. He commutes mostly by ebike and trains his dog in detection work!

Political experience?

No. Startup experience. Looking at metrics, finding consensus, shipping results. The goal would be to hire the best domain experts we can find for critical city leadership. Denver needs visionary leadership. The city is running out of runway to make changes.
I'm not a politician, I see problems. I see possible solutions. I see what is possible for this city if we, collectively, decide to take transformative action.

Hard Conversations to Have

Denver. We have to talk. Our city is in trouble. It's not just us. It's nationwide.

It's our time to admit we have problems. The way to solve those problems is to talk. A terrifying proposal these days, but the truth. So let's do it. I won't talk like a politician here. I'll tell you what I'm about. If you have a question, email me, I'll answer and put it on the site.

The moment the world locked down, everything changed. It's all different. Denver needs to change as quickly as Covid has impacted us.

It'll hurt. We need to admit our roads have gotten away from us. We'll have to lose a few parking spaces. We'll have to admit we need to take radical action on homelessness.

The Revitalization

The cities that thrive will be the ones to notice the world has changed.

  • Infrastructure and a failed vision zero
    • Sweeping and immediate infrastructure adjustments
    • Denver's Infrastructure wasn't designed for the density we have - the time to act is now.
    • A prioritization of the 5280 loop
    • An immediate deployment of protected bikeways/scooterways throughout the city.
    • Traffic easing measures on eligible and problematic streets
    • A reclamation of eligible streets to pedestrian and car-free areas.
    • A citywide initiative to rebuild (or build) our sidewalks.
    • A concerted cleanup effort at transit hubs citywide - zero tolerance in critical transit municipal hubs
  • Regaining Trust in Denver
    • Decryption of Emergency Broadcasts within Denver
    • Comprehensive review of rules of engagement by officers
    • Expanding Foot Patrols, with the same officers, across the downtown area
  • A Comprehensive Approach to Housing Stability
    • Immediate "low-hanging fruit" updates at shelter facilities.
    • Immediate expansion of safe-camping centers.
    • Expansion of eviction defense and emergency rent relief programs.
    • Year over Year rent stabilization capped at prime + tbd
    • Supporting safe parking initiatives
  • Public Safety Reformations
    • Crackdown on Catalytic Converter and Car Thefts - SB-009, and locally Denver PD has presented a bill to the current administration.
    • Once shelter overhauls are complete, Denver will enforce the camping ban. Once we have humane capacity, there will be no tolerance. It is not dignified to die on the street.
    • Reclaimation of treelawns citywide - rocks and fences are dangerous and unsightly. (we have a tree program, please!)
    • Concerted cleanup effort in highly trafficked public areas (Union Station Included).
    • Enforcement of the "camping ban" prioritizing camps that are most linked with threats to public safety.
    • We all deserve to feel safe in our city. We have to admit we have a problem. And we have to act humanely.
  • Fentanyl - a radical approach is required
    • Expansion of safe injection sites throughout Denver
    • Free essential supplies in free vending machines on city property containing testing materials, first aid, sexual health, and drug health.
    • More programming
  • Scooters are here to stay
    • Infrastructure first. Immediate deployments of bikeways/scooterways on already traveled routes.
    • Removing the vendor cap - we need more last mile solutions, not fewer. I miss denver BCycle. I miss cheap scooter and ebike rides.
    • Expansion of scooter/bike parking areas on the bikeways/scooterways
    • Heavier enforcement of proper scooter parking
  • Vision Zero.
    • Has failed.
    • Infrastucture first. Traffic easing measures, dedicated non-car lanes on eligible roads.
    • Light retiming across the Denver Metro
    • Fasttrack 5280 loop
    • Fasttrack Colfax Rapid Transit Corridor
    • Immediate sidewalk repair, replace, and buildout campaign
  • A Changing Downtown
    • Incentivize conversions from office space to mixed use residential space
    • Shifting 16th street busses off of the mall to create a fully pedestrian plaza.
    • Faster city response to campsites.
    • Continue to champion city music events in newly closed city areas.
  • Unleashed Dogs - Adapt to and Regulate
    • Dedicated "barky hour" at public parks with the most offleash occurences in the mornings and evenings. Zero tolerance policy in parks outside of those hours.
    • Higher enforcement priority
  • A changing climate and Denver
    • Expansion of Denver's centralized steam system. Hook up as many buildings to the system as possible.
    • A wide deployment of trees for eligible treelawns throughout the city. Trees reduce ambient temperatures.
    • Greening any new traffic calming measures
    • It is time for green roofs. We're all too hot. We're all in this together.
    • An audit of climate vulnerabilities specific to Denver - from fire threats to flash flooding events to heavy snow events, Denver needs a plan.
  • Emergency Preparedness
    • Citywide plans for every national security emergency possible.
    • Particular attention paid to excessive heat, rain, and localized fire threats
    • Prepare additional cooling centers, make more water refill stations available citywide
    • Extensive crowd-control training by all necessary officials
    • Regular response tests to ensure Denver is ready
  • The Opposition - Let's talk.

    Why do people have political websites only tell you to donate to their campaign. Let's try something new. I'll speak as though you asked.

    • Don't touch the Roads! Denver traffic is bad enough!
      Which is why our streets have gotten away from us. Denver got too big too quickly. We didn't adapt our infrastucture accordingly. We're going to lose some parking spaces. Some streets will become shared and one way. Some streets will be closed entirely. It's a change. But we're going to get through it together. We'll be stronger for it.
    • Scooters don't belong on the sidewalks!
      No. No they do not. Things to consider. 1) Where would you propose the scooters go? [infrastucture first] 2) GPS isn't accurate enough to detect a sidewalk from a bikeway/scooterway. Let's put an end to that debate right now.
    • Where am I going to park? I deserve to park in front of my home!
      The reality is the city changed, it grew. That's why your home is worth more today than it was in 2010.
      We need to accept that as a fact. You may have to park a block away. You may have to pay for parking in certain areas. I pay to park. No one likes it, but we plan too heavily around cars. Maybe you won't vote for me. But I promise you that we can and will make the streets safer through design. Just let us do it.
    • It's a war on cars!
      It is not a war on cars. We are enabling safe alternative transportation. Some people in this city believe scooters are a menace, they are a menace because they currently live somewhere they shouldn't but are forced to. br No one likes finding parking - you drove a few miles to get icecream and now there is no where to park! Imagine having an ebike? Or renting a scooter? Taking a docked bike? Parking is solved.
    • Union Station (insert public space here) is meant for everyone!
      It is. When we use any public space we are signing a social contract. To carry out what you carry in. To treat the park and the people with respect. Union Station, in particular, and the A line should be a magnificent entrance into Downtown Denver. Plainly, it is unacceptable to use these spaces in an untoward way.
      This is why we must support programs such as safe injection sites and overdose prevention centers.
    • Safe Injection Sites are Bad for Denver!
      I never liked the idea. I'll be honest. So, I get it. Let's look at this from 30,000 feet for a second, right? 1) People are doing drugs in public spaces and we'd prefer them not to. 2) People are dying. Drugs will be used, it's an unfortunate reality. Our opeoid crises has also gotten away from us.
      We need to give drug users a safe space to use so they don't have to in the park or the alley by your home.
      We also need to make testing and treatment options freely and widely available.
    • Don't promote bad behaviour!
      Denver, I told you we have a problem. Look around. These things are already being done. We have to accept that. On city property, we're going to install free vending machines with narcan, testing materials, period products, basic birth control and contraceptives, clean needles, basic first aid materials, and meal replacement bars.
      They'll be in city buildings. They're there as a non-judgemental place to get what you need. The machine won't judge you. It won't ask you questions. We all need help. If you are struggling, you deserve to be safe. Especially now.
    • An EBike doesn't belong on the bikelane!
      As long as they go the speed limit (mine has a ClassII mode, it tops out at 15mph) they're bikes. They belong. Scooters are coming too.

      1.6million people used a scooter or bike rental last quarter alone. People are using the bikelanes.
    • How're you gonna pay for the new infra?
      By sidelining I70 projects.
    • We don't trust the police.
      Denver Police have made mistakes. That's not up for debate. Let's tackle trust one at a time.
      1) My priority is to decrypt Denver Emergency dispatch.
      Dispatch is the heart of the city. We all deserve free access to it. You can encrypt if you have a tactical situation, but dispatch is public.
      We need to examine use of force in Denver. Too many people are dying by police intervention. Too many people are getting hurt.
      The city should start to trust officers again, but the officers need to show they should be trusted. In the Golden Triangle, we have a few officers on patrol or stationed. You see the same faces, you might not get their names, but you recognize them.

    • You don't like police!
      I don't trust the police right now. Even I, a tall blonde white dude, have had bad experiences. But that doesn't mean I don't respect the officers who are still on the force.
      This is the hard truth - we have a problem with use of lethal force in the city. Denver Police have not shown their communications department is trustworthy in reporting to the city. This is why we make dispatch public. To begin to trust again.

Any action in regards to budget is designed not to defund anything, but to free Denver Police up to police. Free Denver Fire up to do their incredibly imporant jobs (thanks for saving Dozens on 13th and Cherokee btw).
      It's hard to hear, but it's true. There are problems. We need to address them and we are going to.
    • What will Denver do in a post-Roe world?
      Denver and Colorado are pro-choice. That won't change. We, the city, should work to codify abortion access as healthcare. We need to come to terms with the fact we are at war on a constitutional level.
      Denver doesn't just need to provide for the women of Denver, we need to provide for the women around us. Denver is a constitutional island at the moment. The system will be under stress for the considerable future.
    • Bikes don't follow the rules! They ran a stop sign!
      Behold. The Idaho stop. It's now state law. A stop sign becomes a yield. I commute by bike, I'll explain. 1) Stop sign approaches. It's clear ahead of me. I have a wider field of view than a car. I look left, right. That's clear. I throttle back up and go through. A yield. 2) Stop light approaches. I try to filter up towards the front of the line if possible. Then I stop. I'm at a stop sign. If things clear up and I get a space, I start going. Why do we do this? 1) I see you. I promise you. I don't want to die. 2) I can see more than you. 3) Starting back up is incredibly labor-intensive - it's more efficient. 4) At a light, I can filter up and be at speed by the time you catch up. Safer for everyone! Yes, people bike poorly. People also drive poorly. You've seen as many red lights ran as I have.
    • Downtown sucks.
      Not a question. It's not great. It's really not. We have problems downtown. It has the absolute potential to great. We're going to have to reclaim some roads near high trafficked areas. You'll still get foot traffic. Every city that has reclaimed streets has come to love it. We'll survive and thrive.
      We also need to reclaim some public spaces from urban campsites. This is another infrastucture First policy. Once we build out humane short-term shelter and housing opprotunities for all, the campsite pressure will naturally decrease.
      The world also changed, and downtown hasn't yet. Work from home isn't going away - our downtown needs to convert, rapidly, to residential and mixed use. The city needs to incentiveize downtown tower conversions from office space to residential. It's good for the building owners. It's good for Denver. It's good for housing.
      Some of these changes will be scary. Covid changed our world. It isn't going back. Which is why we need to adapt just as quickly if we want to suvive and thrive during a difficult decade.

      Let me be frank, there are business and developer interests in this city. It's silly to think that only individuals make this city function. I have a normal PAC. I'll probably meet with lobbyists. I'll likely have to have conversations I don't want to have.

      I'm worried. This election is the most important election Denver has had in a long while. The 2030's are a radically different world than the 2020's. We have to rethink things, act decisively, and quickly.
      Why do I think, of all people, I can run a city of this size? Well, I learn quick. I was also really good in Model United Nations in College.

I'll admit, if you vote for me, I'll try to execute things as quickly as I can. If you don't want change or hate bikes, I am not your candidate.

      I am learning about the airport. I'll form a position later. Though, as someone (who used to) fly, I'm not happy. The people aren't happy. And I want answers.
      I also think the A line should be as cheap as possible and safe. Zero tolerance for poor behaviour in this zone. The Airport to the Train to Downtown should be seamless. We want to knock down some I70 traffic? That's a big way to do it.
    • Why does the city need to spend money to plan for a fire that hasn't happened yet?
      I spent my formative years in Manhattan. I lived across from World Trade for a few years. You'd often see NYPD's rapid response units staged. Taking up the entire block. You knew the city knew something. And it made you feel better.
      Fact is, NYC had planned for every possible scenario so they could deploy assets quickly and know what to do under stress.
      Not to discount the Office of Emergency Management - we need to fund those fine people to better protect the city from threats we don't have on our bingo cards.
      I want plans, I want drills, I want better response times. Denver needs to be ready for the next pandemic or, more likely, dangerous heat, widespread severe flooding, of an uncontained fire.
      We also need to plan for a future we are seeing on the horizon. Cyberattacks, long-term power outages, severe supply chain constraints, or domestic terrorism. We need a command center that's fully modernized and prepared for any emergency situation.
      This city has seen first hand what happens when communication fails and situations escalate. That won't happen again.
