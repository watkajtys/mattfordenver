Matt Watkajtys for Denver Mayor

Who? Matt Watkajtys (Wa-Kite-Us) - Candidate Matt?

Matt has rented in the Golden Triangle in Denver since 2017. Born in Upstate, NY - Matt has lived in Toronto, Los Angeles, and Manhattan. He worked as a product development consultant before Covid. During the lockdowns, he applied for unemployment. To make ends meet, he worked at an Amazon Fulfillment center and as a delivery driver. He is currently utilizing the Emergency Rent Relief program in 2021. He commutes mostly by ebike and trains his dog in detection work!

Political experience?

No. Startup experience. Looking at metrics, finding consensus, shipping results.
Denver (D10) needs a vision. I have one.
Matt is an Eagle Scout, a creative, and a startup operator. I was also pretty great at Model United Nations in High School and College.

Why Run?

This is the single most import election cycle in Denver's recent history. I love my neighborhood in the Golden Triangle, and I'm worried about the future of this city.

District 10 is the most urban district in Denver, what I would consider Denver's Core. I've watched my neighborhood grow, decay, grow some more in the short time I've lived here.

I live in D10, I work here, I sit at Levan Deli on 14th and Cherokee with my dog many mornings. I am at Civic Center Park every single day. I love Union Station and the core entrance to our beautiful city.

Hard Conversations to Have

Denver. Our city is having problems. It's not just us. It's nationwide.

Our downtown is threatened. Our communities are threatened. So we need to talk. Let me tell you what I'm about.

The moment the world locked down in 2020, everything changed. Denver needs to change as quickly as Covid has impacted us.

It'll hurt. We need to admit our roads have gotten away from us. We'll have to lose a few parking spaces and shift some roads to one-ways. You'll have to suffer through new traffic patterns. We'll have to take radical action on homelessness and fentanyl. Some of these changes will be scary and sound counter-intuitive. You might not like some of these changes. But they are steps we will have to take to course-correct this city.

It's Not All Bad

Denver, we don't stand up for ourselves enough. We do not give the Denver in Decay people enough pushback - District 10 is incredible. Denver is a cool city. We need to share our Denver Pride.

The Revitalization

Denver, like many cities in America, has had a rough go at it lately. And the next few years will be absolutely critical to surviving and thriving.

The cities that thrive will be the ones to notice the world has changed.

The Opposition - Let's talk.

    Why do people have political websites only tell you to donate to their campaign. Let's try something new. I'll speak as though you asked.

  • Don't touch the Roads! Denver traffic is bad enough!


    Which is why our streets have gotten away from us. Denver got too big too quickly. We didn't adapt our infrastucture accordingly. We're going to lose some parking spaces. Some streets will become shared and one way. Some streets will be closed entirely. It's a change. But we're going to get through it together. We'll be stronger for it.

  • Scooters don't belong on the sidewalks!


    No. No they do not. Scooters have come close to running over my pup many a time. Things to consider. 1) Where would you propose the scooters go? Infrastucture [Mile High Mode Shift] 2) GPS isn't accurate enough to detect a sidewalk from a bikeway/scooterway. Let's put an end to that debate right now. 3) City needs to provide "parking areas" on destination blocks.

  • Where am I going to park? I deserve to park in front of my home!


    The reality is the city changed, it grew. That's why your home is worth more today than it was in 2010.
    We need to accept that as a fact. You may have to park a block away. You may have to pay for parking in certain areas. I pay to park. No one likes it, but we plan too heavily around cars. Maybe you won't vote for me. But I promise you that we can and will make the streets safer through design. Just let us do it.

  • It's a war on cars!


    It is not a war on cars. We are enabling safe alternative transportation. Some people in this city believe scooters are a menace, they are a menace because they currently live somewhere they shouldn't but are forced to.
    No one likes finding parking - you drove a few miles to get icecream and now there is no where to park! Imagine having an ebike? Or renting a scooter? Taking a docked bike? Parking is solved.

  • Union Station (insert public space here) is meant for everyone [unhoused neighbors]!


    It is. However, when we use any public space we are signing a social contract. To carry out what you carry in. To treat the park and the people with respect. Union Station, in particular, and the A line should be a magnificent entrance into Downtown Denver. It is critical municipal infrastructure. Plainly, it is unacceptable to use these spaces in an untoward way.
    This is why we must support programs such as safe injection sites and overdose prevention centers. Redirect people who need help to places that can help them.

  • Safe Injection Sites are Bad for Denver!


    I never liked the idea. I'll be honest. So, I get it. Let's look at this from 30,000 feet for a second, right? 1) People are doing drugs in public spaces and we'd prefer them not to. 2) People are dying. Drugs will be used, it's an unfortunate reality. Our opeoid crises got away from us.
    We need to give drug users a safe space to use so they don't have to in the park or the alley by your home.

    Fentanyl has made its way into just about every drug in Denver. Anyone should be able to get a test kit and NARCAN, free of charge, free of judgement.

On city property, we're going to install free vending machines with narcan, testing materials, period products, basic birth control and contraceptives, clean needles, basic first aid materials, and meal replacement bars.

  • What will Denver do in a post-Roe world?


    Denver and Colorado are pro-choice. That won't change. We need to come to terms with the fact we are at war on a constitutional level.
    Denver doesn't just need to provide for the women of Denver, we need to provide for the women around us. Denver is a constitutional island at the moment. The system will be under stress for the considerable future.

  • Bikes don't follow the rules! They ran a stop sign!


    Behold. The Idaho stop. It's now state law. A stop sign becomes a yield. I commute by bike, I'll explain. 1) Stop sign approaches. It's clear ahead of me. I have a wider field of view than a car. I look left, right. That's clear. I throttle back up and go through. A yield. 2) Stop light approaches. I try to filter up towards the front of the line if possible. Then I stop. I'm at a stop sign. If things clear up and I get a space, I start going.
    Why do we do this? 1) I see you. I promise you. I don't want to die. 2) I can see more than you. 3) Starting back up is incredibly labor-intensive - it's more efficient. 4) At a light, I can filter up and be at speed by the time you catch up. Safer for everyone! Yes, people bike poorly. People also drive poorly. You've seen as many red lights ran as I have.

  • Downtown sucks.


    Not a question. It's not great. It's really not. We have problems downtown. It has the absolute potential to great. We're going to have to reclaim some roads near high trafficked areas. You'll still get foot traffic. Every city that has reclaimed streets has come to love it. We'll survive and thrive.
    We also need to reclaim some public spaces from urban campsites. This is another infrastucture First policy. Once we build out humane short-term shelter and housing opprotunities for all, the campsite pressure will naturally decrease.
    The world also changed, and downtown hasn't yet. Work from home isn't going away - our downtown needs to convert, rapidly, to residential and mixed use. The city needs to incentiveize downtown tower conversions from office space to residential. It's good for the building owners. It's good for Denver. It's good for housing.
    Some of these changes will be scary. Covid changed our world. It isn't going back. Which is why we need to adapt just as quickly if we want to suvive and thrive during a difficult decade.

    I'm worried. This election is the most important election Denver has had in a long while. The 2030's are a radically different world than the 2020's. We have to rethink things, act decisively, and quickly.
    Why do I think, of all people, I can run a city of this size? Well, I learn quick. I was also really good in Model United Nations in College.
    I have a vision. A vision that's been influenced by many conversations. We're going to hire the best people we can find. We're going to expand that vision. I'll admit, if you vote for me, I'll try to execute things as quickly as I can. If you don't want change or hate bikes, I am not your candidate.

  • What about the airport construction cost overages?


    I am learning about the airport. I'll form a position later. Though, as someone (who used to) fly, I'm not happy. The people aren't happy. And I want answers.
    I also think the A line should be as cheap (free) as possible and safe. Zero tolerance for poor behaviour in this zone. The Airport to the Train to Downtown should be seamless. We want to knock down some I70 traffic? That's a big way to do it.

  • Why does the city need to spend money to plan for a fire that hasn't happened yet?


    I spent my formative years in Manhattan. I lived across from World Trade for a few years. You'd often see NYPD's rapid response units staged. Taking up the entire block. You knew the city knew something. And it made you feel better.
    Fact is, NYC had planned for every possible scenario so they could deploy assets quickly and know what to do under stress. Rumor was rapid response could get anywhere in 3 minutes.
    Not to discount the Office of Emergency Management - we need to fund those fine people to better protect the city from threats we don't have on our bingo cards.
    I want plans, I want drills, I want better response times. Denver needs to be ready for the next pandemic or, more likely, dangerous heat, widespread severe flooding, or an uncontained fire.
    We also need to plan for a future we are seeing on the horizon. Cyberattacks, long-term power outages, severe supply chain constraints, or domestic terrorism. We need a command center that's fully modernized and prepared for any emergency situation, frankly - the city should have a command center.
    This city has seen first hand what happens when communication fails and situations escalate. That won't happen again.
